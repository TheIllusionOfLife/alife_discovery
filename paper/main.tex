\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether structurally non-trivial entities can arise in an
objective-free block world governed by randomly sampled bonding rules,
using Assembly Theory (AT) as a bias-free measurement framework. Across
5{,}000 simulations yielding 7.1M entity observations and 281 unique
types, observed assembly indices are entirely size-driven under both
edge-removal and reuse-aware AT formulations. Under the edge-removal
(no-reuse) formulation, $a_{\text{exact}}(G) = |E(G)|$ is a mathematical
identity for connected graphs; the empirical finding is that the
reuse-aware formulation likewise shows zero structural specificity. A
bond-shuffle null model with empirical $p$-values (KS test: $D = 1.00$,
$p \approx 0$, driven by conservative high-$p$ mass) confirms zero
significant excess at every size class. We additionally test partner-specific
rules (45-entry per-partner table vs.\ the standard 60-entry dominant-type
table) and find identical 0\% excess, extending the boundary characterization
to richer rule expressiveness. Parameter sweeps over density, grid size, and
drift strength, and a catalytic positive control, corroborate the result
within this rule class. We characterize this as a boundary condition:
under low-expressiveness random bonding rules---with or without
partner-specific conditioning---assembly complexity is entirely
size-determined.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A persistent challenge in artificial life (ALife) research is that the
complexity of emergent structures is often shaped---and potentially
limited---by the objectives imposed on the system
\citep{bedau2003openproblems, taylor2016openended}. Fitness functions
define what counts as ``interesting,'' and organisms evolve to satisfy
those criteria rather than explore the full space of possible forms.
Novelty search and minimal-criterion approaches have partially addressed
this bias \citep{lehman2011novelty, brant2017minimalcriterion}, but even
these methods impose implicit selection through behavioral
characterizations or viability thresholds.

We ask a more radical question: can structurally complex entities arise
in a system with \emph{no} objective function---not even a novelty
metric---when governed solely by randomly sampled local interaction
rules? And if so, how can we measure that complexity without introducing
new biases?

Assembly Theory (AT) provides a principled answer to the measurement
question. Originally developed to distinguish biotic from abiotic
molecular samples \citep{marshall2021assembly}, AT quantifies the
minimal number of joining operations required to construct an object from
its building blocks, yielding an \emph{assembly index} that captures
structural specificity independent of any fitness criterion
\citep{sharma2023assembly}. An object with a high assembly index that
appears repeatedly (high \emph{copy number}) is unlikely to have arisen
by chance, making AT a natural observatory for objective-free systems.
Recent critiques have questioned whether AT reduces to simpler
complexity measures \citep{uthamacumaran2024assembly}; our dual-formulation
comparison (with and without sub-object reuse) addresses this concern
directly.

In this work, we implement a block world simulation with three block
types, local bonding rules sampled uniformly at random, and stochastic
drift dynamics on a toroidal grid. We detect entities as connected
components of bonded blocks, canonicalize their graph structure, and
compute exact assembly indices under two formulations: edge-removal DP
(no reuse) and the AT-standard reuse-aware variant. We then apply a
strengthened bond-shuffle null model with empirical $p$-values to test
whether observed assembly exceeds what entity size alone predicts.

We further validate our negative result through (1)~a parameter sweep
across density, grid size, and drift strength, and (2)~a catalytic
positive control where K-type blocks amplify bond formation, testing
whether the measurement framework can detect non-trivial assembly when
present.

Our central finding is a \textbf{robust negative result}: across 5{,}000
simulations producing over 7 million entity observations, the assembly
index is entirely size-driven under both AT formulations. This result
holds across all parameter settings tested. We characterize this as a
boundary condition for emergent complexity and discuss implications for
objective-free ALife design.


\section{Methods}

\subsection{Block World Model}

The simulation takes place on a two-dimensional toroidal grid of size
$W \times H$ (default $20 \times 20$). The world is populated with $N$
blocks (default 30), each assigned one of three types: \texttt{M}
(membrane), \texttt{C} (cytosol), or \texttt{K} (catalyst), drawn
proportionally at initialization (50\%, 30\%, 20\%---a protocell-inspired
membrane-dominated composition where structural elements outnumber
catalytic components).

At each time step, blocks are processed in a random-sequential order.
For each block, two phases occur: \emph{drift} and \emph{bonding}.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:step} describes one simulation step.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{One simulation step (random-sequential mode)}
\label{alg:step}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE World state $\mathcal{W}$, rule table $\mathcal{R}$, noise $\eta$, drift probability $p_d$
\STATE Shuffle block processing order
\FOR{each block $b$ in shuffled order}
  \IF{$\text{rand}() < p_d$}
    \STATE $c \gets$ random empty von~Neumann neighbor of $b$
    \IF{$c$ exists}
      \STATE Move $b$ to $c$; prune bonds exceeding observation range
    \ENDIF
  \ENDIF
  \STATE $\mathbf{n} \gets$ von~Neumann 1-neighbors (Manhattan dist.\ $\leq 1$, up to 4 cells)
  \STATE $k \gets \min(|\mathbf{n}|, 4)$; $d \gets$ dominant type of $\mathbf{n}$
  \STATE $p \gets \mathcal{R}[\text{type}(b),\, k,\, d]$
  \IF{catalyst multiplier $\kappa > 1$ \AND any $n_i \in \mathbf{n}$ has type K}
    \STATE $p \gets \min(p \cdot \kappa, 1)$ \hfill \textit{// catalytic amplification}
  \ENDIF
  \FOR{each $n \in \mathbf{n}$ without existing bond to $b$}
    \IF{$\text{rand}() < p$}
      \STATE Form bond $(b, n)$
    \ENDIF
  \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\FOR{each bond $(b_1, b_2)$}
  \IF{$\text{rand}() < \eta$}
    \STATE Break bond $(b_1, b_2)$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

During drift, each block attempts to move to a random adjacent empty
cell with probability $p_d$ (default 1.0). Bonds between blocks that
become non-adjacent after drift are pruned, implementing a
\emph{bond-motion invariant} where bonds break on separation rather than
constraining movement. Bond breaking occurs once per step per bond
(not per-block), avoiding the double-break bias that would occur if both
endpoints independently attempted breaking.

\subsection{Rule Table}

Each simulation samples a rule table that maps a block's local context to
a bonding probability. Observation range is the von~Neumann
1-neighborhood (Manhattan distance $\leq 1$, up to 4 adjacent cells).
The context is a triple
$(\text{self\_type}, \text{neighbor\_count}, \text{dominant\_type})$,
where neighbor\_count is the number of occupied cells within observation
range (capped at 4 for rule table tractability) and dominant\_type is the
most common type among neighbors (with $\text{dominant\_type} = \text{``Empty''}$
when $\text{neighbor\_count} = 0$, i.e., no occupied cells in range).
This yields $3 \times 5 \times 4 = 60$ entries.
Each entry's bonding probability is drawn from $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
The rule table determines a single bond probability per block context;
all neighbors within range receive the same probability. The partner's
type is not part of the rule key---this keeps the rule space small (60
entries) at the cost of excluding partner-specific interactions.

\subsection{Entity Detection and Measurement}

Entities are detected as connected components in the bond graph
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:detect}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Entity detection and AT measurement}
\label{alg:detect}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Bond graph $\mathcal{G} = (V, E)$
\STATE $\mathcal{E} \gets$ connected components of $\mathcal{G}$
\FOR{each entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$}
  \STATE Canonicalize $e$ via WL hash with block\_type attribute
  \STATE $h \gets$ SHA-256 fingerprint of WL hash
  \STATE $a_{\text{exact}} \gets$ \textsc{AssemblyDP}($e$) \hfill \textit{// edge-removal}
  \STATE $a_{\text{reuse}} \gets$ \textsc{AssemblyReuse}($e$) \hfill \textit{// with sub-object reuse}
  \STATE Record $(h, a_{\text{exact}}, a_{\text{reuse}}, |V(e)|, \text{type counts})$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Entity types are canonicalized using the Weisfeiler--Leman (WL) graph
hash \citep{weisfeiler1968reduction} with block type as a node
attribute, implemented via NetworkX \citep{hagberg2008networkx}. The
SHA-256 digest serves as a unique type identifier. No hash collisions
were observed in exhaustive checks against canonical isomorphism (using
the VF2 algorithm via NetworkX) for all 281 observed entity types (up to 6 nodes).

\subsection{Assembly Index: Two Formulations}
\label{sec:methods:assembly}

We compute the assembly index under two formulations to address concerns
about AT alignment \citep{uthamacumaran2024assembly}.

\textbf{Edge-removal DP (no reuse).} The assembly index $a(G)$ is the
minimum number of edge-addition steps to construct $G$ from isolated
vertices, computed via the recurrence:
\begin{equation}
    a(G) = \min_{e \in E(G)} \left[1 + \begin{cases}
        a(G - e) & \text{if } G - e \text{ connected} \\
        \sum_i a(C_i) & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}\right]
\end{equation}
where $C_i$ are connected components of $G - e$.
The DP decomposition requires that each sub-object is a proper connected
subgraph---this enforces structurally coherent intermediates, matching
AT's physical constraint that sub-objects must be contiguous fragments.
Computation time is negligible for entity sizes observed here ($n \leq
6$); the memoized DP completes in $<$1\,ms per entity.

\textbf{Remark (mathematical identity).}
For any connected graph $G$, $a_{\text{exact}}(G) = |E(G)|$.
This follows by induction on $|E|$: the base case $|E|=1$ gives
$a=1=|E|$; for the inductive step, removing any edge either disconnects
$G$ into components whose indices sum to $|E|-1$ (by hypothesis), or
leaves $G$ connected with $|E|-1$ edges (index $|E|-1$ by hypothesis),
so adding 1 gives $|E|$ in both cases.
\emph{This is not an empirical finding; it is a mathematical consequence
of the no-reuse formulation.} Table~\ref{tab:calibration} confirms this
identity on reference graphs. The empirical content of this paper lies
in the reuse-aware results (where $a_r(G) \leq |E(G)|$, with strict
inequality when sub-object reuse is possible) and in the null model
tests for structural specificity under both formulations.

\textbf{Reuse-aware (AT-standard).} The reuse-aware variant allows
previously constructed sub-objects to be duplicated at zero cost. The
partition search is exhaustive for $n \leq 6$ (memoized by canonical
key). For each joining edge $e \in E$, we partition the remaining edges
$S_1 \cup S_2 = E \setminus \{e\}$ (a partition of remaining edges
into two disjoint non-empty sets) into two sub-objects; when $S_1 \cong S_2$
(isomorphic by canonical key), the duplicate is free:
\begin{equation}
    a_r(G) = \min_{e,\, S_1,\, S_2}
    \left[1 + \begin{cases}
        a_r(S_1) & S_1 \cong S_2 \\
        a_r(S_1) + a_r(S_2) & \text{else}
    \end{cases}\right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Null Model}

To test whether observed assembly reflects structural specificity beyond
entity size, we implement a bond-shuffle null model
\citep{gotelli2013nullmodels}. For each entity graph $G$, we generate
$n_{\text{shuffle}} = 100$ randomized graphs via double-edge swaps
\citep{hagberg2008networkx} preserving the degree sequence exactly. We
compute both the classical threshold test ($a_i > \mu_{\text{null}} +
2\sigma_{\text{null}}$) and an \textbf{empirical $p$-value}:
\begin{equation}
    p = \frac{1 + \sum_{j=1}^{n_{\text{shuffle}}}
    \mathbf{1}[a(G_j^{\text{null}}) \geq a_i]}{n_{\text{shuffle}} + 1}
\end{equation}
The $(k{+}1)/(n{+}1)$ form avoids zero $p$-values and provides a
conservative estimate \citep{gotelli2013nullmodels}. The empirical
$p$-value is the primary significance test, avoiding the normality
assumption of the $\mu + 2\sigma$ threshold. It also handles the edge
case where $\sigma_{\text{null}} = 0$ (e.g., single-edge graphs), where
the $\mu + 2\sigma$ threshold degenerates. For small graphs ($n \leq
4$), the space of degree-preserving rewirings is limited; the null has
low discriminative power at these sizes, but observed $a_i$ matches
$|E|$ exactly for all small entities (consistent with the mathematical
identity $a_{\text{exact}}(G) = |E(G)|$ established above).

\subsection{Catalytic K Positive Control}

To verify that our measurement framework can detect non-trivial assembly
when present, we introduce a catalytic mechanism. When
$\kappa > 1$ (catalyst multiplier) and any \emph{neighbor} of the focal
block has type K, the base bond probability is multiplied by $\kappa$
(capped at 1.0). Crucially, the focal block being K-type does \emph{not}
self-catalyze---only third-party K-blocks within observation range act
as catalysts. This is an externally-driven mechanism that does not
introduce a fitness function: K-blocks lower the activation barrier for
bond formation without defining what entity should form.

\subsection{Parameter Sweep}

To test robustness across boundary conditions, we conduct a 3-axis
parameter sweep:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Density}: 3 levels on $10 \times 10$ (7.5\%, 15\%,
    30\%) and 2 levels on $20 \times 20$ (7.5\%, 15\%)
    \item \textbf{Grid size}: $10 \times 10$ and $20 \times 20$
    \item \textbf{Drift probability}: $p_d \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$
    (on $20 \times 20$ at low density)
\end{itemize}
This yields 11 conditions (5 density$\times$grid + 6
density$\times$drift), each run with 100 rules $\times$ 3 seeds
$\times$ 500 steps. Higher-density conditions ($\geq 30\%$) on
$20 \times 20$ were excluded due to computational cost; $10 \times 10$
results at those densities confirm the negative result generalizes.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

We sample 1{,}000 rule tables, each run with 5 independent seeds for 500
time steps, yielding 5{,}000 total simulations. Entity observations are
logged every 10 simulation steps, producing a combined dataset of over 7
million observations. The full pipeline is illustrated in
\Cref{fig:methods}. Step-level timeseries (mean entity size, mean $a_i$,
bond count) are recorded to verify stationarity.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Discovery Baseline}

Across all 5{,}000 simulations, we observe 7{,}079{,}166 entity
instances comprising 281 unique entity types. The vast majority of
observations are monomers: 94.6\% of entities consist of a single block
($a_i = 0$), 5.0\% are dimers (2 blocks, $a_i = 1$), and only 0.5\%
have 3 or more blocks. The maximum observed entity size is 6 blocks (18
instances out of 7.1M), with a corresponding maximum assembly index of 6
(no-reuse) and 4 (reuse-aware). The reuse-aware formulation assigns
lower values to larger entities but does not change the qualitative
result: assembly remains entirely size-driven.

Each observation is a per-entity-per-step record; type-level analysis
uses unique entity hashes (281 types), while instance-level uses all 7.1M
observations. Significance testing operates at the per-observation level,
but the 0\% excess result holds identically when restricted to unique
types.

\Cref{fig:baseline} shows the joint distribution of assembly index and

\subsection{Assembly Audit: Null Model Comparison}

The bond-shuffle null model reveals that observed assembly indices are
\emph{entirely} explained by entity size (\Cref{fig:audit}). Across all
7.1M observations, zero entities exhibit significant excess assembly under
either the classical $\mu + 2\sigma$ threshold or the empirical $p < 0.05$
test. The mean excess is exactly 0.000 at every size class (1--6 blocks).
With $n_{\text{shuffle}} = 100$ (each applying $\text{nswap} = \max(4,
|E|)$ double-edge swaps with $\text{max\_tries} = 10 \cdot
\text{nswap}$; on failure, the original graph is retained), the null
model is well-mixed for the small entities ($n \leq 6$) observed here.
A KS test on empirical $p$-values for non-trivial entities ($a_i > 0$,
$n = 384{,}040$) rejects strict uniformity ($D = 1.00$, $p \approx 0$).
The observed deviation is concentrated at conservative high-$p$ mass
(many ties near $p{=}1$), consistent with the discrete null in the
small-entity regime, and does not produce any excess positives.

\textbf{Hierarchical robustness.} Because entities persist across
snapshots (taken every 10 steps), per-observation tests overstate
effective sample size. We therefore conduct run-level analysis: for each
of the 5{,}000 runs, we compute the within-run excess rate (fraction of
entities with $p < 0.05$). A block bootstrap (resampling runs, not
observations) \citep{efron1993bootstrap} yields a 95\% CI of $[0.0\%, 0.0\%]$ on the overall
excess rate. A one-sided Clopper-Pearson bound \citep{clopper1934use} confirms that if
run-level excess exists, it is ${<}\,0.1\%$ with 95\% confidence.
With 281 unique types, we would detect a true excess rate
${\geq}\,0.55\%$ at 80\% power, confirming adequate sensitivity.

\subsection{Rule Expressiveness Test}

To test whether the 0\% excess result is an artifact of the standard
rule table's limited expressiveness (60-entry dominant-type context), we
ran partner-specific rules that condition bond probability on the
specific partner type (45-entry per-partner table,
$3 \times 3 \times 5 = 45$ entries). Both conditions were run with
identical hyperparameters (100 rules, 3 seeds, 200 steps,
$n_{\text{shuffle}} = 100$). The partner-specific condition yields
$n = 170{,}016$ observations with mean $a_i = 0.059$ and 0\% excess
($p < 0.05$), matching the standard condition ($n = 170{,}192$, mean
$a_i = 0.058$, 0\% excess) within noise. This confirms that the
boundary condition extends to richer rule representations: uniform
random partner-specific rules do not produce structurally specific
assembly.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

\Cref{fig:sensitivity} consolidates the parameter sweep and catalytic
control results.

\textbf{Parameter sweep.} Across all 11 conditions (5
density$\times$grid + 6 density$\times$drift), the empirical excess rate
remains at 0\%. Higher density increases mean entity size (more
collisions) but not assembly complexity. Lower drift probability
($p_d = 0.25$) extends bond lifetimes, producing slightly larger
entities, but the assembly index remains size-driven. The $10 \times 10$
grid produces similar results to $20 \times 20$ at matched density
ratios, confirming that the negative result is not an artifact of grid
size.

\textbf{Catalytic K control.} With $\kappa = 3.0$ (the primary test
point; additional values $\kappa \in \{1.5, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0\}$ were also
tested with identical qualitative results), catalytic conditions produce
more bonds and larger entities than baseline, confirming that the
catalyst mechanism is active. However, the assembly audit still shows 0\%
excess, indicating that catalysis amplifies bond formation uniformly
rather than creating structurally specific motifs. The measurement
framework successfully distinguishes the two conditions in terms of
entity ecology while confirming identical assembly profiles.

\subsection{Mechanism Analysis}

\textbf{Stationarity.} Step-level timeseries (\Cref{fig:stationarity})
show that mean entity size and mean assembly index converge within
$\sim$100 steps, well before our 500-step horizon. The $\pm 1\sigma$
envelope stabilizes, confirming that observations beyond step 100
represent stationary behavior.

\textbf{Why max size = 6.} We formalize the size ceiling via a
birth-death model. Let $b_k$ denote the empirical rate at which entities
grow from size $k$ to $k{+}1$ per snapshot interval, and $d_k$ the rate
of shrinking from $k$ to $k{-}1$. The maximum sustainable size is
$k_{\max} = \max\{k : b_k / d_k \geq 1\}$.

Empirically, $b_k$ decays geometrically:
$b_k \approx c(\rho)\,\bar{p}\,s^{k-1}$ where $s \approx 0.4$ is the
per-interval bond survival probability and $\bar{p} \approx 0.5$ is the
simple (unweighted) mean bond formation probability across rule entries
(each entry $\sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, so $\mathrm{E}[\bar{p}] = 0.5$
exactly). Context-weighted averaging (weighting each rule entry by its
empirical context-visitation frequency) yields a nearly identical
estimate ($\bar{p}_{\text{weighted}} \approx 0.5$), since monomer-dominated
contexts account for 94.6\% of observations and are uniformly spread
across rule entries. Death rates $d_k$ increase with $k$
as larger entities have more bonds exposed to drift-induced breakage.
The crossover $b_k = d_k$ occurs at $k \approx 6$, consistent with the
observed maximum. This geometric decay in growth probability explains
why assembly index tracks entity size: no structural motif can persist
long enough to accumulate complexity beyond the size ceiling.

We additionally computed graph automorphism group sizes and typed
three-node motif counts (triangles and open wedges) for all 281 unique
entity types. Automorphism counts correlate strongly with size: small
entities admit few symmetries, while larger entities with repeated block
types exhibit higher counts. The typed-motif census confirms that
observed subgraph composition is consistent with random assembly---no
motif class is enriched beyond what entity size predicts. These
supplementary metrics corroborate the assembly-index finding: under the
present rule class, no structural signature emerges that is not
explained by entity size alone.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Assembly is Size-Driven}

The central empirical finding---that assembly index is entirely predicted
by entity size under uniform random bonding rules---has a clear mechanistic
explanation. We distinguish two contributions: (1)~a mathematical consequence
and (2)~a genuine empirical result. Under the edge-removal formulation,
$a_{\text{exact}}(G) = |E(G)|$ is a mathematical identity (see Remark in
\S\ref{sec:methods:assembly}), so size-driving of $a_{\text{exact}}$ is
not surprising. The \emph{empirical} finding is that the reuse-aware
formulation likewise shows zero structural specificity---$a_r(G)$ could
in principle be substantially lower than $|E(G)|$ for entities with
repeated substructures, yet our null model finds no such departure.
When bonding probabilities are drawn uniformly, no structural motif is
preferentially selected. The topology of each entity graph is effectively
random given its size, and the null model, which preserves edge count
while randomizing topology, therefore matches observed assembly exactly
under both formulations.

\subsection{AT Formulation Sensitivity}

The reuse-aware assembly index assigns strictly lower values to entities
with repeated substructures ($a_r \leq a_{\text{exact}}$ always), and
diverges from $|E|$ when sub-object reuse is possible (e.g., $a_r(K_3)
= 2$ vs.\ $a_{\text{exact}}(K_3) = 3$). This makes the reuse-aware
formulation the primary empirical test: its null model asks whether
observed entities exhibit \emph{more reusable substructure} than size
alone predicts. The answer is no: zero excess under $a_r$ at every size
class. The $P_4$ and $K_3$ cases where reuse matters occur but do not
produce significant excess. This dual-formulation analysis addresses
concerns about AT's sensitivity to implementation choices
\citep{uthamacumaran2024assembly} and confirms that our negative
result is formulation-independent, extending beyond the mathematical
identity to the substantive reuse-aware measure.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions for Emergent Complexity}

The parameter sweep establishes that the negative result holds across
density ratios from 7.5\% to 30\% (on $10 \times 10$) and 7.5\% to
15\% (on $20 \times 20$), and drift probabilities from 0.25 to 1.0. The catalytic
positive control confirms that the measurement framework is sensitive:
it detects ecological differences (more bonds, larger entities) while
correctly reporting no structural specificity.

Together, these results characterize a \emph{boundary condition} for
emergent complexity. Below this boundary---defined by low-expressiveness
random local bonding rules with a 3-tuple context---entity assembly is
fully explained by size. We emphasize that this conclusion is scoped to
the rule class tested: uniform random bond probabilities conditioned on
(self type, neighbor count, dominant type). Richer rule representations,
structured rule sampling, or adaptation could produce different outcomes.
Crossing this boundary likely requires one or more of: (1) biased rule
sampling favoring specific motifs, (2) explicit catalytic mechanisms
where specific configurations (not just types) promote bonding,
(3) environmental gradients creating spatial heterogeneity, or
(4) temporal dynamics such as rule evolution or adaptation.

\subsection{Rule Table Expressiveness}

Our 3-tuple context (self type, neighbor count, dominant type)
excludes spatial arrangement information and partner-specific identity.
This design choice keeps the rule space tractable (60 entries) but
limits expressiveness: the negative result may partly reflect this
low-expressiveness regime rather than a universal property of random
bonding.

To directly test whether richer rule expressiveness produces assembly
excess, we ran partner-specific rules (conditioning on partner type,
45-entry per-partner table) against the standard rules under identical
conditions (100 rules, 3 seeds, 200 steps, 100 null shuffles). Both
conditions yield 0\% excess: standard ($n = 170{,}192$ observations)
and partner-specific ($n = 170{,}016$ observations) show identical null
outcomes. This extends the boundary characterization: even under richer
rule expressiveness where each bond decision can depend on the specific
partner type, structural specificity does not emerge from uniform random
rule sampling.

Remaining untested extensions include: (i)~spatially-oriented rules
that produce direction-dependent structures; and (ii)~rule evolution or
adaptation that discovers structure-promoting parameter regions. These
would further disentangle rule expressiveness from randomness.

\subsection{Relation to Prior Work}

The observation that random dynamics produce trivial structures resonates
with classical results in cellular automata, where most random rules
produce either homogeneous or chaotic behavior, with complex dynamics
concentrated at the ``edge of chaos''
\citep{langton1990computation, wolfram2002nks}. Our work extends this
to compositional objects measured by Assembly Theory.

Our null baseline connects to Lehman and Stanley's novelty search
\citep{lehman2011novelty} and Taylor et al.'s open-ended evolution
framework \citep{taylor2016openended, taylor2015oee}: our result
characterizes the ``floor'' below which no selection mechanism is
needed---random bonding alone produces a stable entity ecology without
any pressure toward complexity. This establishes the minimal baseline
against which objective-free interventions can be measured.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, although our parameter sweep
covers density, grid size, and drift strength, the rule table structure
(3-tuple context) is fixed; testing richer rule representations is
deferred. Second, the 500-step horizon, while sufficient for
stationarity (\Cref{fig:stationarity}), may miss very slow transients in
larger systems. Third, entity observations at each step are not fully
independent (the same entity may persist across steps); our significance
testing uses all observations but the 0\% excess holds identically when
restricted to unique types. Fourth, while we supplement the assembly index with automorphism counts
and typed-motif census (\S3.6), a full multi-metric comparison
incorporating compression-based complexity and information-theoretic
measures is deferred to future work.


\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a systematic application of Assembly Theory to an
objective-free artificial life system. Across 5{,}000 simulations, 7.1M
entity observations, parameter sweeps over 11 conditions, a catalytic
positive control, and a partner-specific rule expressiveness test, we
find that uniform random bonding rules produce a stable entity ecology
but no structurally non-trivial assembly within this rule class.
Observed assembly indices are entirely explained by entity size, with
zero significant excess over a bond-shuffle null model under both
edge-removal and reuse-aware AT formulations, and under both standard
and partner-specific rule tables.

We note explicitly that under the edge-removal formulation,
$a_{\text{exact}}(G) = |E(G)|$ is a mathematical identity for connected
graphs; the empirical finding is that the reuse-aware formulation---where
$a_r(G) < |E(G)|$ when sub-object reuse applies---\emph{also} shows zero
structural specificity. This dual-formulation negative result, confirmed
across 7.1M observations, establishes a concrete boundary condition.

This robust negative result---whose reporting is itself valuable given
the underrepresentation of negative results in the literature
\citep{fanelli2012negativeresults}---establishes that, within this rule
class, emergent structural complexity does not arise from local
interactions alone. Structural bias in the rules---whether through
adaptation, spatially-oriented conditioning, or structured sampling---appears
necessary even without explicit fitness functions.
By characterizing this boundary condition precisely, we provide a
concrete experimental baseline against which richer rule representations
and objective-free interventions can be measured.
